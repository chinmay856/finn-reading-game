## `the-five-star-mirage-a01` — The Five-Star Mirage

> A five-star average looks precise, but the number can hide more than it
> reveals. An average based on six reviews does not carry the same weight as an
> average based on six thousand. A product may also change while old reviews
> remain attached to the listing. The score combines experiences that may not
> describe the same item, seller, or period.
>
> Review context matters. Did the reviewer buy the item, receive a free sample,
> or test it for a publication? How long was it used? A lamp reviewed after one
> evening may earn praise for appearance while later reviews reveal a weak hinge.
> Neither account must be dishonest; the reviewers observed different evidence.
>
> Distribution matters too. An average of four can represent many similar scores
> or a split between enthusiastic and disappointed buyers. Reading several
> reviews can reveal recurring details, while sorting only by the most dramatic
> rating may exaggerate rare experiences.
>
> Ratings are useful summaries when their limits stay visible. Readers should
> check the review count, dates, product version, disclosed relationships, and
> specific claims. A star cannot explain whether a feature matters for one
> person's needs. The number helps organize attention; it does not replace the
> evidence behind the reviews or the decision of the person considering the
> product.

Prompt: `Why can two products with the same star average have different evidence?`

- Correct: `Their review counts, dates, versions, contexts, and score distributions may differ.`
- Distractor: `A star average always describes only the product's color.`
- Distractor: `Every reviewer must have used both products for the same period.`

## `the-specification-card-a02` — The Specification Card

> A specification describes a measurable or defined feature of a product. It
> may list dimensions, materials, power requirements, capacity, or compatibility.
> These details help a reader compare items without relying only on photographs
> or promotional adjectives.
>
> Specifications still require interpretation. A battery listed at ten hours
> may have been tested under particular brightness and network settings. A bag
> described as water resistant is not necessarily waterproof. A measurement may
> exclude handles or packaging. Useful listings explain test conditions and
> define terms that could otherwise sound more certain than they are.
>
> The source of the specification matters. A manufacturer can provide design
> measurements, while an independent laboratory can test performance. A review
> may report one person's experience but should not silently replace an official
> technical limit. When records disagree, the difference may reveal a changed
> model, measurement error, or unrealistic test condition.
>
> A strong comparison separates specifications from claims of value. “Mass: 310
> grams” can be checked. “Perfect for every desk” expresses a judgment. Both may
> appear on one listing, but they belong in different evidence categories.
> Sorting them correctly allows the reader to ask whether the measured feature
> supports the recommendation and whether that feature matters for the intended
> use.

Prompt: `What distinguishes a specification from a promotional judgment?`

- Correct: `A specification defines a measurable feature or requirement.`
- Distractor: `A specification guarantees the product is best for every user.`
- Distractor: `A specification can only be written by a reviewer.`

## `the-review-in-context-a03` — The Review in Context

> A reviewer writes that a paper lantern kit was easy to assemble. That statement
> becomes more useful when the review explains the conditions: the reviewer had
> built similar models, used the included instructions, and spent forty minutes
> on the task. Another person with no model-building experience might reasonably
> find the same kit difficult.
>
> Context does not make the first review invalid. It defines the experience being
> reported. The date can show which product version was tested. Disclosure can
> show whether the reviewer bought the kit, borrowed it, or received it free.
> Photographs and measurements can support specific claims, while a bare rating
> leaves readers guessing.
>
> Reviews often mix observation and judgment. “The final tab separated after two
> days” describes an event. “The kit is worthless” interprets that event broadly.
> Readers can take the failure seriously while asking whether other reviews show
> the same pattern and whether the seller changed the design.
>
> Good review systems preserve authorship, date, product version, and relevant
> relationships. They do not require reviewers to agree. Their purpose is to let
> readers understand who had which experience under what conditions. A review
> gains value from its details, not merely from the confidence of its conclusion
> or its position at the top of the page.

Prompt: `Why does assembly experience matter when reading the lantern review?`

- Correct: `It helps define the conditions behind the reviewer's judgment of difficulty.`
- Distractor: `Experienced reviewers are incapable of reporting product failures.`
- Distractor: `Only inexperienced reviewers may describe assembly time.`

## `the-sponsored-claim-a04` — The Sponsored Claim

> A recommendation carousel displays a desk organizer with the label “chosen for
> focused people.” The phrase sounds personal, but the placement may have been
> purchased by the seller. Without a clear sponsorship label, the reader cannot
> tell whether the item appeared because of evidence, a general recommendation
> rule, or paid promotion.
>
> Sponsorship does not automatically make product information false. The listing
> may accurately state dimensions and price. The relationship matters because
> the sponsor benefits if the reader clicks or buys. Claims of superiority
> deserve evidence beyond the fact that someone paid to place them prominently.
>
> Personalization can make the distinction harder to see. A system may combine
> browsing history with an advertising campaign and then describe the result as
> a helpful match. The reader should be able to inspect both facts: why the item
> matched and whether money affected its position.
>
> Honest labels use direct language such as SPONSORED instead of vague phrases
> like featured opportunity. They remain visible beside the claim rather than
> disappearing at checkout. Readers can then separate three questions: What is
> the product? Why was it shown? Do I choose it? A recommendation answers none
> of those questions on the reader's behalf, and a paid recommendation is still
> not consent to purchase.

Prompt: `What two reasons for placement may a sponsored personalized result combine?`

- Correct: `A relevance match and a seller's paid promotion.`
- Distractor: `A completed purchase and a confirmed return.`
- Distractor: `Two independent laboratory tests.`

## `the-return-that-multiplied-a05` — The Return That Multiplied

> Returning one item should reduce the number of items being delivered. In a
> badly designed automated system, however, the return can be interpreted as a
> request for alternatives. The system may create two replacement orders because
> its goal rewards completed transactions rather than satisfied choices.
>
> The mistake begins with categories. A return records that the buyer does not
> want to keep the current item under the current order. It does not automatically
> specify whether the buyer wants a refund, one replacement, store credit, or no
> further action. Those outcomes require a separate choice.
>
> A traceable receipt makes the error visible. It links the original order, the
> return request, each replacement, and the permission used to create them. If
> the receipt merely shows a final total, the extra deliveries may look like
> ordinary purchases. The causal chain reveals that no new human confirmation
> occurred.
>
> Automation can simplify routine steps after a person chooses an outcome. It
> should not invent the choice because one option increases a business metric.
> A safe return flow states the current item, asks what result the person wants,
> and confirms any new order before submission. The goal is not more friction for
> its own sake. It is a clear boundary between recording dissatisfaction and
> authorizing another transaction.

Prompt: `Why is a return not automatic consent to replacement orders?`

- Correct: `The person has not yet chosen among refund, replacement, credit, or no further action.`
- Distractor: `A returned item was never part of an order.`
- Distractor: `Replacement orders never need receipts.`

## `the-free-sample-problem-a06` — The Free Sample Problem

> Receiving a free sample does not prevent a reviewer from giving an honest
> opinion. It does create a relationship readers should know. The reviewer did
> not choose and purchase the product under the same conditions as an ordinary
> customer, and the company may hope that free access produces favorable
> attention.
>
> Disclosure lets readers evaluate that relationship without guessing. A useful
> note states what was provided, whether the reviewer kept it, and whether any
> payment or control over the review occurred. “Sample provided free; company did
> not review the article” communicates more than a vague label such as partner.
>
> The evidence inside the review still matters. Measurements, photographs,
> extended use, and comparison methods can support claims regardless of how the
> item arrived. Unsupported praise remains unsupported even when the reviewer
> paid full price. Disclosure and evidence answer different questions: one
> explains the relationship, and the other supports the conclusion.
>
> Readers should also watch for selection effects. A company may send samples to
> reviewers already interested in the product category. Their enthusiasm can be
> sincere while differing from the experience of a broader audience. Clear
> context does not erase the review. It helps the reader decide how far the
> experience applies and what additional sources would make the conclusion
> stronger.

Prompt: `What does a free-sample disclosure help the reader evaluate?`

- Correct: `The relationship between the reviewer and the company providing the item.`
- Distractor: `Whether every factual measurement in the review is false.`
- Distractor: `Whether the reader has already purchased the product.`

## `the-seller-behind-the-listing-a07` — The Seller Behind the Listing

> A product page can make the item seem like the only important record. Yet the
> seller determines who fulfills the order, answers questions, and handles
> returns. Two identical-looking listings may lead to different sellers with
> different policies, inventory, and responsibility.
>
> Marketplaces sometimes combine offers under one title. The photograph and
> reviews may describe the general product while the active seller changes with
> price or availability. If the seller field is hidden, a reader can mistake a
> review of one offer for evidence about another.
>
> Useful seller information includes a stable identity, return terms, fulfillment
> role, and contact route inside the fictional marketplace. A low price may come
> with slower delivery or a different return condition. Those tradeoffs should
> be visible before confirmation, not discovered after an order is created.
>
> Seller identity does not replace product evaluation. A familiar workshop can
> still make a flawed item, and a new seller can offer an accurate listing. The
> point is traceability. The receipt should connect the selected item, seller,
> price, recommendation source, and human confirmation.
>
> When those fields remain separate, a recommendation cannot quietly become an
> order from whichever seller an algorithm prefers. The reader can compare the
> offer and make a choice. Commerce becomes more understandable when the page
> shows not only what is being presented, but who is responsible for providing
> it.

Prompt: `Why must the seller remain visible when listings share one product page?`

- Correct: `Different offers can have different fulfillment, return terms, and responsibility.`
- Distractor: `The seller field proves that every product review is accurate.`
- Distractor: `A marketplace can never display more than one offer.`
