# Nine-site sample passage sampler

## Status

These are review-only first samples for the nine proposed sites beyond
WikiWhy. They test whether each site's reading identity feels genuinely
different before anyone builds a full deck. They are not wired into the
prototype and have not yet passed the repository's full passage acceptance
gate or a real-microphone transcription test.

Only the passage under each **Reading text** heading would appear in the
Reading Companion and be speech-scored. Site copy, section labels, source
notices, and questions stay outside scored text.

Eight reading texts are newly written adaptations or syntheses. ViewTube uses
an authentic public-domain literary excerpt with Wells's words preserved;
surrounding speech quotation marks are omitted so the selection stands alone.
Source and rights links were checked on July 11, 2026. Project Gutenberg's
public-domain determinations apply in the United States; distribution elsewhere
requires a separate jurisdiction check.

## ThreadIt — Why Disagreement Matters

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; argumentative
nonfiction; approximately 288 words.

### Reading text

A discussion can feel successful when everyone agrees, but agreement is not
proof that a claim is true. John Stuart Mill argued that an unpopular opinion
has value even before we know whether it is correct. If the opinion turns out
to be true, silencing it prevents people from correcting a mistake. If it
contains only part of the truth, debate can reveal what the popular view has
ignored. Even a thoroughly mistaken argument can serve a purpose: it forces
people to explain the evidence behind their own beliefs.

Without that pressure, a true idea may survive only as a slogan. People repeat
its conclusion but forget the reasoning that gives it meaning. They may know
which side they support without understanding why. Mill therefore urged
readers to examine an opposing case in its strongest form. It is not enough to
hear a weak summary supplied by someone who already rejects it. A serious
thinker should be able to state the other position fairly, identify its best
evidence, and then explain where it fails.

This principle does not mean every claim deserves equal confidence. Evidence
still matters, and harmful deception does not become useful merely because it
attracts attention. The point is that judgment improves through disciplined
comparison rather than applause, outrage, or repetition. Before joining a
confident majority, a reader can ask three questions: What evidence supports
this claim? What evidence would count against it? Have I understood the best
objection?

Disagreement becomes productive when it changes the goal from defeating a
person to testing an idea. The result may be a changed opinion, a stronger
argument, or an honest admission that the evidence remains incomplete. Each
outcome is more valuable than certainty borrowed from a crowd.

### Content record notes

- **Source:** John Stuart Mill, *On Liberty*, Chapter II, “Of the Liberty of
  Thought and Discussion,” [Project Gutenberg eBook 34901](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/34901).
- **Rights basis:** The item is marked public domain in the United States.
  [Project Gutenberg permissions](https://www.gutenberg.org/policy/permission)
  explains that U.S. public-domain items may be republished and adapted. Do not
  include the Gutenberg wrapper or trade on its name.
- **Adaptation:** Contemporary original synthesis of Mill's arguments about
  truth, partial truth, unsupported dogma, and hearing the strongest opposing
  case.
- **Question:** According to the passage, how can a thoroughly mistaken
  argument still improve a discussion?
- **Answer:** It can force people to explain and test the evidence behind their
  own beliefs, preventing a true idea from becoming an unsupported slogan.
- **Sensitivity:** Abstract references to censorship, majorities, and
  deception; deliberately nonpartisan.
- **Speech review:** `John Stuart Mill` is the only proper name. Test
  `Stuart/Stewart` as an accepted recognition equivalent.

## FacePlace — A Second Reading

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; literary retelling and
analysis; approximately 292 words.

### Reading text

Elizabeth had been proud of her ability to judge character. One man appeared
warm, witty, and open; Darcy seemed distant and arrogant. Because the first man
treated her with attention and Darcy did not, she accepted a story that
confirmed what she already wanted to believe.

Then Darcy gave her a letter. It challenged several events as she understood
them and offered details she could compare with her own memories. On the first
reading, anger guided her. She dismissed the account because it threatened her
confidence in herself. But she read it again. This time, instead of asking which
man she liked, she asked which explanation fit the evidence.

Small contradictions began to matter. The charming storyteller had avoided
questions that should have been easy to answer. Some of his choices did not
match the honorable image he presented. Darcy's manner was still unpleasant,
but an unpleasant manner did not prove a dishonest character. Elizabeth also
noticed that she had interpreted every new fact in a way that protected her
first impression.

The discovery embarrassed her because her mistake was not simple ignorance.
She had enjoyed thinking of herself as unusually perceptive. Praise from one
person and rejection from another had influenced what she considered
believable. The letter did more than change her opinion of two men; it exposed
the vanity inside her judgment.

A first impression is not useless. Tone, behavior, and appearance can provide
clues. Yet a clue is not a complete account of a person. Better judgment
requires time, independent evidence, and a willingness to reread the story when
facts refuse to fit. Elizabeth's most important correction was not deciding
that one person was entirely good and another entirely bad. It was recognizing
that confidence can arrive before understanding.

### Content record notes

- **Source:** Jane Austen, *Pride and Prejudice*, primarily Chapters 35–36,
  [Project Gutenberg eBook 1342](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1342).
- **Rights basis:** The item is marked public domain in the United States; see
  [Project Gutenberg permissions](https://www.gutenberg.org/policy/permission).
- **Adaptation:** Original retelling and analysis of Elizabeth's response to
  Darcy's letter. It preserves the reasoning turn, not Austen's phrasing.
- **Question:** What does Elizabeth do differently during her second reading
  of the letter?
- **Answer:** She compares its explanation with remembered evidence and
  contradictions instead of judging it according to which person she prefers.
- **Sensitivity:** Historical courtship and embarrassment. The source scene's
  attempted-elopement and financial-coercion details are omitted.
- **Speech review:** Test `Darcy/D'Arcy`; normalize `reread/re-read` and
  possessive apostrophes.

## MyCorner — A Cabin with a Purpose

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; reflective nonfiction;
approximately 294 words.

### Reading text

A writer once left town to live in a small cabin beside a pond. He did not
claim the woods were magical, and he did not intend to disappear from society
forever. The move was an experiment. He wanted to remove enough noise and
routine to see which parts of life were essential and which had merely become
habits.

The cabin made each choice visible. A limited room could not hold endless
furniture. A simple schedule could not absorb every invitation, purchase, or
piece of news. With fewer distractions, ordinary experiences demanded closer
attention: the sound of rain on the roof, the changing surface of water, the
work required to prepare a meal, and the thoughts that arrived when no one else
supplied a subject.

Simplicity, however, was not the same as deprivation. The purpose was not to
suffer, win a contest, or prove that everyone should live in the same way. The
writer wanted to spend deliberately. Money was one kind of cost, but time and
attention were costs as well. An object might be inexpensive yet demand hours
of care. A busy habit might seem harmless while quietly deciding how every
afternoon would be used.

The experiment led to a harder question: How much of a person's life is chosen,
and how much is inherited from expectation? A room can answer part of that
question. What someone places within reach reveals what they expect to do. What
they remove creates space for another possibility.

Eventually, the writer left the cabin. Staying forever would have turned one
deliberate choice into another automatic routine. The value of the experiment
was not the building itself. It was learning to examine the structures—rooms,
schedules, and habits—that shape attention before attention silently shapes a
life.

### Content record notes

- **Source:** Henry David Thoreau, *Walden*, especially “Where I Lived, and What
  I Lived For” and the concluding explanation for leaving,
  [Project Gutenberg eBook 205](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/205).
- **Rights basis:** The item is marked public domain in the United States; see
  [Project Gutenberg permissions](https://www.gutenberg.org/policy/permission).
- **Adaptation:** Original modern synthesis of deliberate living, attention,
  simplicity, and the risk that any chosen path can become routine.
- **Question:** Why does the writer eventually leave the cabin?
- **Answer:** Remaining forever would turn an intentional experiment into
  another automatic routine; the lesson was to examine choices, not preserve
  one lifestyle permanently.
- **Sensitivity:** Solitude and material simplicity; no meaningful concern in
  this adaptation.
- **Speech review:** No proper names in scored text. Test `deprivation` and
  `deliberately`; normalize em dashes for alignment.

## Yahuh! Portal — The Newspaper That Found People on the Moon

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; historical journalism
and media literacy; approximately 241 words.

### Reading text

In August eighteen thirty-five, readers of a New York newspaper encountered an
astonishing report. A powerful new telescope, the story claimed, had revealed
forests, lakes, bison, goats, and winged people on the Moon. The articles
borrowed the name of a respected British astronomer and pretended to come from
a scientific journal. Neither claim was true.

The report arrived in six installments. Each new part added vivid detail and
ended with another discovery, giving readers a reason to buy the next issue.
Other newspapers repeated the story, so its reach grew faster than anyone
could verify its origin. Repetition made the account look widely confirmed,
even though many versions traced back to the same source.

The writer later described the series as satire aimed at extravagant theories
about life on the Moon. Yet satire only works as satire when readers can
recognize the joke. In this case, the scientific style, the real astronomer's
reputation, and the public's excitement about new telescopes made the fiction
plausible.

The episode is not merely a story about gullible people in the past. It
demonstrates how presentation can imitate evidence. A careful reader would ask
who actually made the observation, where the original report appeared, whether
the named expert confirmed it, and whether independent observers could inspect
the same facts. A crowded news portal may display ten headlines, but ten copies
of one claim do not become ten sources. The number of repetitions measures
attention, not truth.

### Content record notes

- **Sources:** Richard Adams Locke, *The Moon Hoax*,
  [Project Gutenberg eBook 62779](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/62779), with
  facts cross-checked against the Library of Congress essay
  [“Peoples & Creatures of the Moon”](https://www.loc.gov/collections/finding-our-place-in-the-cosmos-with-carl-sagan/articles-and-essays/life-on-other-worlds/peoples-and-creatures-of-the-moon/).
- **Rights basis:** The underlying 1835 work is marked public domain in the
  United States. The Library of Congress link is used for factual verification,
  not copied prose.
- **Adaptation:** Original explanatory account of the newspaper series. Proper
  names stay in metadata rather than scored text.
- **Question:** Why did publication in many newspapers fail to provide
  independent confirmation?
- **Answer:** The repeated versions traced back to one report. Repetition
  increased attention and apparent credibility, not independent sources.
- **Sensitivity:** Historical deception and fanciful creatures only. Do not
  import archaic or offensive wording from the original articles.
- **Speech review:** The year is written in words. No personal names, dialect,
  acronyms, or unusual symbols occur in scored text.

## ViewTube — The Sky Becomes a Streak of Fire

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; authentic
science-fiction narration; approximately 255 words.

### Reading text

I am afraid I cannot convey the peculiar sensations of time travelling. They
are excessively unpleasant. There is a feeling exactly like that one has upon
a switchback—of a helpless headlong motion! I felt the same horrible
anticipation, too, of an imminent smash. As I put on pace, night followed day
like the flapping of a black wing.

The dim suggestion of the laboratory seemed presently to fall away from me,
and I saw the sun hopping swiftly across the sky, leaping it every minute, and
every minute marking a day. I supposed the laboratory had been destroyed and I
had come into the open air. I had a dim impression of scaffolding, but I was
already going too fast to be conscious of any moving things. The slowest snail
that ever crawled dashed by too fast for me.

The twinkling succession of darkness and light was excessively painful to the
eye. Then, in the intermittent darknesses, I saw the moon spinning swiftly
through her quarters from new to full, and had a faint glimpse of the circling
stars.

Presently, as I went on, still gaining velocity, the palpitation of night and
day merged into one continuous greyness; the sky took on a wonderful deepness
of blue, a splendid luminous colour like that of early twilight; the jerking
sun became a streak of fire, a brilliant arch, in space; the moon a fainter
fluctuating band; and I could see nothing of the stars, save now and then a
brighter circle flickering in the blue.

### Content record notes

- **Source:** H. G. Wells, *The Time Machine*, Chapter IV, “Time Travelling,”
  [Project Gutenberg eBook 35](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/35).
- **Rights basis:** The item is marked public domain in the United States; see
  [Project Gutenberg permissions](https://www.gutenberg.org/policy/permission).
- **Text treatment:** Authentic 255-word excerpt using Wells's words and British
  spelling. The opening and closing speech quotation marks are omitted for a
  standalone display; no prose is modernized or paraphrased.
- **Question:** Which images show that time is passing increasingly fast for the
  narrator?
- **Answer:** Day and night flap past, the sun hops by once per minute, the moon
  races through its phases, and finally the sun and moon appear as continuous
  bands rather than separate objects.
- **Sensitivity:** Motion sickness, dread, and fear of a possible collision;
  no injury occurs in the excerpt.
- **Speech review:** Authentic vocabulary raises intentional difficulty. Test
  `switchback`, `scaffolding`, `intermittent`, `palpitation`, `greyness`,
  `luminous`, and `fluctuating`. British spelling does not change pronunciation.

## Search-ish — Two Results, One Suspicious Message

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; paired-source reasoning
and digital literacy; approximately 247 scored words. The `Source A` and
`Source B` labels are not speech-scored.

### Reading text

**Source A: Consumer protection guidance**

An urgent message can look convincing because familiar details are easy to
copy. A fake notice may borrow a company name, imitate its colors, and describe
a problem with an account or payment. Those details create recognition, but
they do not prove who sent the message. The strongest clues often concern
behavior instead: the contact is unexpected, the sender demands immediate
action, or a link asks for a password or financial information. The safer
response is not to click its links or open its attachments. Find the
organization's real website or phone number independently, and ask whether the
request is genuine. Verification should begin outside the message being tested.

**Source B: Law-enforcement guidance**

A targeted email scam may be more careful than an obvious fake. Criminals can
study public information, imitate a normal business request, or change one
letter in an address. Some enter a real conversation and wait until a payment
is expected before sending false instructions. Correct grammar and accurate
background details therefore do not establish authenticity. A sudden change in
account information, payment method, or timing deserves a second channel of
confirmation. The person receiving the request should call a known number or
speak to the supposed sender directly, rather than replying through the same
email thread.

Read together, the sources support a broader research rule: appearance is weak
evidence of origin. When a message asks for sensitive information or
irreversible action, confirm both the source and the request through a path the
message itself does not control.

### Content record notes

- **Sources:** Federal Trade Commission,
  [“How To Recognize and Avoid Phishing Scams”](https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/how-recognize-avoid-phishing-scams),
  and Federal Bureau of Investigation,
  [“Business Email Compromise”](https://www.fbi.gov/how-we-can-help-you/scams-and-safety/common-frauds-and-scams/business-email-compromise).
- **Rights basis:** The [FTC website policy](https://www.ftc.gov/policy-notices/website-policy)
  says most agency-authored material is U.S. Government work in the public
  domain and requests attribution. The [FBI privacy policy](https://www.fbi.gov/privacy-policy)
  permits distribution or copying of unattributed Bureau information while
  prohibiting presentation that implies FBI approval. No seals, logos, or
  endorsement claims are used.
- **Adaptation:** Original paired synthesis of two independently published
  federal sources.
- **Attribution:** “Source facts adapted from consumer-protection guidance by
  the United States Federal Trade Commission and Federal Bureau of
  Investigation. No endorsement implied. No claim to original U.S. Government
  works.”
- **Question:** What rule do both sources support, and what distinct evidence
  does Source B add?
- **Answer:** Both recommend verification through a separately known contact
  path. Source B adds that sophisticated scams may imitate business routines,
  alter one letter in an address, or enter an existing payment conversation.
- **Sensitivity:** Defensive discussion of fraud and theft; no victim names,
  loss amounts, threats, or actionable offensive instructions.
- **Speech review:** Keep source labels outside scoring. Normalize
  `email/e-mail`; URLs, agency abbreviations, and names stay in metadata.

## Amaze-On — The Five-Star Mirage

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; consumer-information
essay; approximately 271 words.

### Reading text

A star rating looks like a fact. Four point eight seems precise, as if thousands
of buyers measured a product with the same instrument. In reality, the number
is a summary of many claims, and those claims can differ in quality.

Imagine you are choosing a portable speaker. One listing has hundreds of
glowing comments posted within two days. Several reviewers have no history, and
many repeat the same unusual phrase. These clues do not prove fraud, but they
should slow you down. A sudden burst of reviews may have been organized. An
account created for a single review may not represent an ordinary customer.
Even a review that sounds moderate can be fake; someone trying to appear
believable might choose four stars instead of five.

The direction of the rating is not proof either. A seller may purchase praise
for its own product, while a dishonest competitor may post negative comments
about a rival. A real customer may also receive a free item or payment and fail
to disclose it. The words on screen may be genuine, biased, or invented, and
appearance alone may not reveal which.

The sensible response is not to distrust every review. It is to treat reviews
as evidence that needs context. Compare several sources. Look for expert
testing from organizations with clear methods. Check when an account was
created, what else it reviewed, and whether the platform labels incentives.
Most important, separate popularity from reliability. A crowded scoreboard can
tell you what people appear to think. It cannot, by itself, tell you why they
think it or whether every voice belongs to a real buyer.

### Content record notes

- **Source:** Federal Trade Commission,
  [“How To Evaluate Online Reviews”](https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/how-evaluate-online-reviews).
- **Rights basis:** The [FTC website policy](https://www.ftc.gov/policy-notices/website-policy)
  says most FTC website material is U.S. Government work in the public domain,
  requests attribution, bars deceptive use, and warns that some third-party
  material remains protected. This adaptation uses agency prose only, excluding
  graphics and user submissions.
- **Adaptation:** Original hypothetical synthesis of review bursts, account
  history, fake positive and negative reviews, incentives, and comparison.
- **Attribution:** “Source facts adapted from consumer guidance by the United
  States Federal Trade Commission. No endorsement implied. No claim to original
  U.S. Government works.”
- **Question:** Why does the author treat a star rating as a starting point
  rather than proof?
- **Answer:** It summarizes claims of unequal reliability that may be biased or
  manipulated, so a buyer needs context and other evidence.
- **Sensitivity:** Commercial fraud and manipulation without a real retailer,
  product, or victim.
- **Speech review:** Numbers are written as words. `Incentives` is the only
  moderately uncommon token.

## Spotty-Fi — The City That Learned to Improvise

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling; cultural-history essay;
approximately 274 words.

### Reading text

It is tempting to tell the history of music as a row of labeled boxes: one
genre ends, another begins, and a famous performer starts each new era. Jazz
resists that tidy story. Its early development in New Orleans grew from
encounters among people, places, and traditions over time.

The city had been governed by France and Spain before becoming part of the
United States. Its neighborhoods included communities with roots in Europe,
Africa, the Caribbean, and elsewhere. Music filled parades, churches, clubs,
streets, and social gatherings. Brass-band traditions met the offbeat pulse of
ragtime, the emotional structure of the blues, African American spirituals,
and rhythms carried across the Atlantic. No single influence simply replaced
another. Musicians borrowed, answered, altered, and combined what they heard.

Improvisation made this exchange audible. Instead of reproducing every note
exactly, a player could make choices during a performance while listening
closely to the band. One phrase might invite a response from a trumpet, drum, or
voice. The audience also shaped the event through movement and reaction. A
performance became a conversation rather than a recording played the same way
every time.

This creativity developed within a segregated society. Jazz attracted listeners
across racial lines, but its popularity did not erase the barriers faced by
Black musicians and communities central to the music. Remembering both facts
matters: jazz demonstrates cultural exchange, and its history also records
inequality.

A music app can classify a track in seconds. Understanding the track takes
longer. A genre label is useful, but it is only a doorway. Beyond it are
migrations, neighborhoods, conflicts, experiments, and people listening
carefully enough to make something new together.

  says NPS-created material is generally U.S. public domain unless otherwise

- **Adaptation:** Original synthesis reframed around genre labels,

- **Answer:** The label identifies a category, but understanding the music

## MapGuess — A Map Is Not a Photograph

**Intended band:** Grades 10–12 pending formal leveling;
science-and-technology explanation; approximately 284 words.

### Reading text

A map can look permanent. Its lines are clean, its labels are confident, and
its symbols seem to declare that the world has been measured and settled. A
nautical chart reveals why that impression is misleading.

Unlike a road map, a nautical chart is designed for travel across water. It may
show shoreline shape, water depth, rocks, wrecks, floating markers, anchorages,
and regulated areas. A navigator uses this information to plan a route, so a
missing detail can matter. Yet a chart cannot display everything. Cartographers
combine measurements from water-depth surveys, shoreline teams, the Coast
Guard, and other sources. They review the quality of the data, decide which
features belong on the chart, and choose how to represent them without making
the page unreadable.

Those choices are not the only reason charts change. The physical world changes
too. Rivers deposit silt, which can reshape a coast or make a channel shallower.
Storms move sand, damage structures, and wash debris into waterways. Human
activity adds another layer. Crews dredge harbors. Officials move navigation
markers. Builders add or remove piers, bridges, cables, and pipelines.
Recommended routes and regulated zones can also be revised.

For these reasons, an accurately made old chart may still be dangerous to use
now. The cartographer did not necessarily make a mistake; the conditions
changed after the work was published. A responsible navigator asks more than,
“Does this map look official?” The better questions are: What purpose was it
made for? Which information does it include? When was it updated? What evidence
supports it?

Every map is a useful reduction of a complicated place. Reading one well means
noticing both what it shows and what it had to leave out.

### Content record notes

- **Source:** NOAA National Ocean Service,
  [“What Is Nautical Cartography?”](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/education/tutorial_nautical_charts/nautical_charts03_cartography.html).
- **Rights basis:** NOAA is the named author. The [National Ocean Service reuse
  FAQ](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/about/faq.html) says most NOAA information
  is public domain unless noted, requests NOAA credit, and prohibits implied
  endorsement or presentation of modified work as official. Do not reuse NOAA
  images or marks without item review.
- **Adaptation:** Original argument using NOAA's facts about chart purpose, data
  selection, environmental change, and human alterations.
- **Attribution:** “Source facts adapted from NOAA's National Ocean Service. No
  endorsement implied. No claim to original U.S. Government works.”
- **Question:** How can an old chart be both accurately made and dangerous to
  use now?
- **Answer:** It may have represented conditions correctly when published, but
  storms, sediment, construction, markers, or regulations may have changed.
- **Sensitivity:** Wrecks and storms without injuries or disaster detail.
- **Speech review:** Test `nautical`, `cartographers`, `anchorages`, and `dredge`.
  `Floating markers` deliberately avoids the common `buoys/boys` recognition
  ambiguity.
