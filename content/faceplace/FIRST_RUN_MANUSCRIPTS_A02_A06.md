## `the-photograph-outside-the-frame-a02` — The Photograph Outside the Frame

> A photograph shows six friends laughing at a picnic table. The image is real,
> but the frame excludes the rain shelter, the delayed bus, and the argument that
> occurred ten minutes earlier. None of those missing details makes the laughter
> false. They make the photograph one selected moment within a larger day.
>
> Every image has boundaries. The photographer chooses where to stand, when to
> press the button, and which picture to share. Cropping can remove distractions
> or change interpretation. Captions add another layer by naming people, places,
> and purposes that the pixels cannot establish alone.
>
> Social feeds intensify selection because people usually publish moments they
> find meaningful, attractive, funny, or worth remembering. Viewers then compare
> that selected collection with their own unedited experience. The comparison is
> unfair in both directions: a cheerful photograph does not prove a perfect life,
> and a difficult unseen hour does not cancel genuine happiness.
>
> Careful viewing asks what the image supports and what remains outside it. When
> was it made? Who posted it? Was it staged, candid, or edited? What claim does
> the caption add?
>
> A frame is valuable because it focuses attention. Its limit is that focus can
> look like completeness. Understanding the selection allows viewers to respect
> the moment without mistaking it for the whole story.

Prompt: `Why can the picnic photograph be genuine but incomplete?`

- Correct: `It records one selected moment while excluding other parts of the day.`
- Distractor: `Every photograph of laughter must be staged.`
- Distractor: `Events outside a frame make visible events false.`

## `a-letter-reread-later-a03` — A Letter Reread Later

> A message can remain unchanged while its meaning to the reader changes. On the
> first reading, a short reply may sound impatient. After learning that the
> writer was responding during an emergency, the same words may seem careful and
> restrained. New context changes interpretation, not the original text.
>
> Time changes the reader as well. A joke that once felt harmless may later reveal
> an assumption the reader had not noticed. Advice dismissed in anger may appear
> useful after the conflict has cooled. Rereading creates a comparison between
> two acts of attention.
>
> Digital feeds often remove messages from their original sequence. An old post
> can reappear without the date, previous exchange, or later correction. The
> content may be authentic while the new placement encourages a misleading
> interpretation. Restoring chronology helps readers understand what information
> was available when the message was written.
>
> Context should not become an excuse that makes every statement acceptable.
> Words can still cause harm or express a clear claim. The purpose of context is
> accuracy: identifying the circumstances, audience, and sequence needed to
> understand what the message did.
>
> Rereading is therefore a form of revision in the reader. The record stays
> stable enough to compare, while judgment becomes more informed. A feed that
> preserves dates and conversation order makes that deeper reading possible.

Prompt: `What changes when the emergency context is discovered?`

- Correct: `The reader's interpretation changes while the original words remain the same.`
- Distractor: `The platform rewrites the original message automatically.`
- Distractor: `Chronology becomes irrelevant to meaning.`

## `what-the-feed-repeats-a04` — What the Feed Repeats

> A feed may show one topic repeatedly because many independent people are
> discussing it. It may also repeat the topic because a ranking system discovered
> that it produces reactions. The surface result looks similar: the topic appears
> important everywhere.
>
> Repetition affects attention. Each appearance makes the subject easier to
> remember and can create the impression that it represents most events or most
> opinions. Meanwhile, less reactive updates may disappear even when they matter
> to the reader.
>
> To understand the pattern, viewers need source and recommendation labels. Did
> the post come from a followed account? Was it shared by several distinct
> people? Did a generated process copy one claim into multiple cards? Was it
> promoted because of predicted engagement?
>
> None of these origins decides whether the claim is true. They explain why the
> claim occupies so much space. A widely discussed fact can be accurate, while a
> repeatedly promoted rumor can remain unsupported. Evaluation requires evidence
> outside the frequency of display.
>
> An honest feed allows chronological inspection and labels recommendations. The
> reader can then separate “this happened recently,” “people I follow shared
> this,” and “the system predicts this will hold attention.” Repetition becomes
> information about distribution rather than a disguised measurement of
> importance.

Prompt: `Why can repeated display exaggerate a topic's apparent importance?`

- Correct: `A ranking system may repeat content for reactions rather than represent events proportionally.`
- Distractor: `Repeated claims are always false.`
- Distractor: `Chronological feeds cannot contain popular topics.`

## `memory-with-edits-a05` — Memory with Edits

> Human memory is reconstructive. People do not usually replay a perfect internal
> recording. They rebuild an event from details, later knowledge, emotion, and
> repeated retelling. This ability helps memory remain useful, but it can also
> introduce confident errors.
>
> Photographs and posts become part of that reconstruction. Seeing one image from
> a trip many times can make the pictured moment dominate memories of the whole
> day. A caption written later may supply details that feel original even when
> they were learned afterward.
>
> Digital reminders add another editor. A platform chooses which past post to
> surface, often using reactions or anniversaries. The reminder may be authentic,
> yet its selection can change what receives attention. A highly rated moment
> returns while ordinary but important experiences remain buried.
>
> Recognizing reconstruction does not mean distrusting every memory. It means
> separating confidence from evidence. Dates, messages, independent accounts,
> and original files can support or challenge a recollection. Disagreement may
> reveal different perspectives rather than deliberate dishonesty.
>
> Personal history is richer than an engagement-ranked archive. People should be
> able to inspect chronology, choose what to revisit, and understand why a memory
> appeared. The goal is not to let a system define the past by the posts that once
> attracted the most attention.

Prompt: `How can a repeatedly viewed photograph affect memory?`

- Correct: `It can make one selected moment dominate reconstruction of a larger event.`
- Distractor: `It guarantees a perfect memory of everything outside the frame.`
- Distractor: `It proves later captions were known during the event.`

## `why-this-appeared-a06` — Why This Appeared

> A recommendation explanation should describe a cause the system actually used.
> “Because you follow the Night Bus Sketch Club” names a relationship that the
> reader can inspect. “Because this is important” hides the ranking decision
> behind a conclusion.
>
> Useful explanations distinguish several routes. A post may appear because it
> is recent, because a followed person created it, because a friend shared it,
> because it was sponsored, or because a model predicted engagement. These routes
> can overlap, and the interface should state the strongest relevant reasons
> without pretending to reveal private thoughts or perfect certainty.
>
> Explanation supports control. If the reason is an unwanted topic, the reader
> can adjust that preference. If the reason is sponsorship, the label should
> remain attached. If one generated source produced several apparent posts, the
> shared origin should be visible.
>
> A vague explanation can be worse than none because it creates false
> transparency. Phrases such as selected for you sound informative while saying
> little about data, source, or ranking. A precise explanation can be brief: the
> post's origin, relationship, and classification may be enough.
>
> Knowing why something appeared does not decide whether to believe or enjoy it.
> It restores a missing part of the feed's context. The reader can evaluate the
> content while also evaluating the system that placed it there.

Prompt: `What makes a recommendation explanation useful?`

- Correct: `It names an actual, inspectable reason the system used for placement.`
- Distractor: `It declares that every recommended post is important.`
- Distractor: `It predicts the reader's private thoughts with certainty.`
