# Internet Recovery 98 campaign spine and mission hub

## Status and purpose

This is a review-ready story and interaction design for connecting the ten site
campaigns into one game. It is not an implementation specification and it does
not replace any site's own three-act design. The worked WikiWhy campaign and
the nine proposed site campaigns remain independently authored.

Visual reference:
[`campaign-spine-and-hub-v3.png`](../../apps/internet-recovery/art/concepts/campaign-spine-and-hub-v3.png).

The spine answers five questions:

1. What does Finn see between site repairs?
2. How can he choose what to repair without facing ten options at once?
3. How does Chinmay's helpful-CEO performance crack in a flexible site order?
4. What does each secured site contribute to the larger story?
5. How do ten finished sites lead to the separate final breach and
   **Smarter Than the Developer** conclusion?

## Core campaign decision

Global story turns are triggered by the **number of secured sites and evidence
files**, not by a fixed order of site names.

WikiWhy is the onboarding site because it is already the worked prototype.
After that, most of the remaining sites can arrive in different orders. Each
site keeps its local apparent problem, midpoint change, progress fiction, and
resolution. The campaign spine only changes the surrounding context: what Amy
currently suspects, how Chinmay behaves, and what the recovered evidence means.

This gives the story a reliable shape without forcing FacePlace to reuse
WikiWhy's percentages, making MapGuess depend on ThreadIt, or turning every
replay into the same list.

## What Finn is actually doing

Finn is not earning permission to read the next worksheet. He is operating the
only stable recovery system left on the network.

At the campaign level, every secured site does three things:

- restores one funny, recognizable piece of the broken Internet;
- adds one site-specific evidence file to the Case File;
- reveals one fragment of the outside write path that keeps changing repairs.

The evidence files make careful reading matter to the story. No single file
proves sabotage. Together, they reveal the route Chinmay's systems use to alter
sites and eventually attempt entry into Internet Recovery 98.

## The recovery desktop hub

The hub is Amy's trustworthy Internet Recovery 98 desktop, visibly contained
inside the modern browser page. It remains stable during the ten ordinary site
campaigns. The Internet viewed through its browser is broken; the recovery
desktop itself is not.

### Primary desktop elements

| Element | Purpose | Player-facing behavior |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Recovery Browser** | Select and revisit sites | Opens a simple recovery-map homepage rather than ten permanent browser tabs. |
| **Incoming Cases** | Keep choice manageable | Shows at most three unsecured sites at once after WikiWhy. A completed case is replaced by another unsecured case. |
| **Case File** | Carry the global mystery | Holds ten visible evidence slots. Each secured site fills one slot with a distinct artifact and one piece of the access-route diagram. |
| **Amy Support** | Give concise context | Amy notices patterns, explains tools, and prepares containment. She does not choose answers or complete repairs. |
| **Reading Companion** | Preserve the core mechanic | Minimized on the hub; opens beside the corrupted site during a reading. It is never mixed into decorative site copy. |
| **Techno** | Comedy and optional visual cues | Wanders the safe desktop with her orange-and-blue ball, reacts to new files, and may point toward the next useful window without becoming required navigation. |
| **Taskbar/status area** | Make the place feel persistent | Shows open recovery tools, genuine connection state, current site status, and optional sound controls. |

### Site states

The hub uses only three durable site states:

- **Incoming** — available as a current case;
- **Recovering** — local campaign state has begun and can be resumed;
- **Secured** — the site's ending has been reached and Chinmay's ordinary
  write path can no longer undo it.

There is no failure-locked state. A difficult reading does not remove a case or
revoke a completed repair. Site-specific progress can advance in larger or
smaller visible steps, but every accepted reading produces a real consequence.

### Why only three incoming cases

Ten simultaneous icons read like a level-select menu. Three active cases feel
like a live desktop receiving problems. The selection can be drawn from all
remaining sites while honoring basic content-variety rules, such as avoiding
three literary passages in a row. Finn can finish a started case or choose one
of the other incoming cases without losing progress.

The full Recovery Map still shows all ten sites as silhouettes, recovering
icons, or secured icons. It communicates the size of the job without making
every silhouette feel like a locked school assignment.

## Campaign flow

### Boot sequence — establish the trustworthy space

Internet Recovery 98 connects through a short dial-up-inspired interstitial
that reflects real loading and adds no artificial delay. Amy identifies the
software as an old-code clean room. Chinmay appears as a confident, slightly
ridiculous CEO who says the Internet experienced a temporary issue caused by
“Chinmay's AI,” as though its creator were unrelated.

The desktop contains one incoming case: WikiWhy.

Important tone rule: nothing here tells Finn that a person intentionally broke
the site. WikiWhy looks confidently wrong because the Internet is broken.
Chinmay's involvement initially reads as ordinary adult incompetence.

### Site one — learn the loop, not the conspiracy

WikiWhy teaches the practical rhythm:

1. open a corrupted page;
2. read the independent Reading Companion passage;
3. watch the site visibly repair;
4. survive the site's own midpoint reversal;
5. secure the page and receive an evidence file.

The first evidence file appears mundane: a recovery log with a strange
timestamp. Amy files it without accusation. Chinmay congratulates Finn in the
tone of an executive claiming credit for someone else's successful fix.

### One to three secured sites — Routine Recovery

After WikiWhy, three Incoming Cases appear. Site order is flexible. Chinmay
remains available, upbeat, and plausibly helpful. His explanations are wrong in
funny ways, but the game does not yet announce malicious interference.

Each evidence file looks like a different technical leftover: a duplicated
source ID, future-dated shipping label, moved destination pin, identical media
hash, promoted-feed record, or template overwrite. In isolation, any could be
part of the original collapse.

After the third secured site, Techno rolls her ball across the open Case File.
The three route fragments align. Amy notices the same unusual timing pattern in
all of them:

> “These broke in different ways, but something touched all three after you
> started fixing them. Keep going. I want more than a weird coincidence.”

This is curiosity, not a villain reveal.

### Four to six secured sites — Background Changes

The next site-specific midpoint changes occur in a new context: Amy is watching
for live writes. She still lets each site's local story do the revealing. A
page may duplicate one source, replace a queue, move a target, or apply one
profile to everyone; the global story does not impose one sabotage effect.

Chinmay's explanations become more strained. He blames caching, personalization,
engagement, “legacy browser feelings,” and other phrases that sound technical
without matching the evidence.

At six secured sites, the Case File has enough fragments to trace the changes to
an outside administrator route associated with Chinmay's company systems. Amy
does not revoke it yet because the route is distributed across the remaining
sites and an incomplete block could teach the system how to bypass the clean
room.

> “You were right to keep the repair logs. This isn't old damage. Something is
> rewriting finished work from outside. I can contain it when we have the whole
> route.”

Finn now understands that someone is actively changing his repairs. The game
still allows Chinmay one last beat of plausible denial.

### Seven to nine secured sites — The Helpful Act Cracks

Chinmay becomes visibly reactive. He interrupts site campaigns, changes his
story, and starts describing Finn's recovery work as unauthorized optimization.
His systems are still capable enough to create real obstacles; the joke is his
haste and overconfidence, not that he is harmless.

Amy's Case File changes from a folder of curiosities into an access-route map.
Every secured site closes one route segment. The remaining segments are visible
but not assigned to a predetermined site, so whichever campaigns remain can
carry the late-game pressure.

After nine sites, the map shows one outside path still open. Amy says she can
prepare a revocation but cannot safely apply it until the last site's evidence
confirms where the path terminates.

### Ten secured sites — the apparent finish

The final ordinary site can be any remaining site. Its local three-act campaign
still ends on its own terms. Returning to the hub fills the tenth evidence slot,
closes the access-route diagram, and shows a clear `10 OF 10 SITES SECURED`
celebration.

The game pauses long enough for that accomplishment to feel real. Techno gets
her ball; Amy says the Internet repairs are stable; Chinmay's outside site writes
are denied. This is an apparent campaign finish, not a fake reward instantly
snatched away.

Then an unexpected file arrives in a previously nonexistent eleventh slot:

`LIVE_EVIDENCE_11 — SOURCE: RECOVERY DESKTOP`

It was created by the attempted breach itself, not recovered from another site.

### Final breach — the one time the safe desktop breaks

The attack violates a rule the player has learned across the whole campaign:
Internet Recovery 98 has always been trustworthy. Site windows close. The safe
desktop flashes red, icons gain obviously fictional corruption, and Chinmay's
helpful-CEO performance ends.

This is not an eleventh website and should not simply repeat a site's progress
bar. The capstone asks Finn to use three accumulated ideas:

1. **Trace origin** — distinguish ten apparent warnings from the one outside
   route producing them.
2. **Preserve evidence** — keep the live file and the ten recovered logs while
   quarantining the attempted write.
3. **Revoke access** — finish the final recovery reading and independently
   verify the assembled route, then execute the revocation command Amy prepared
   and safeguarded.

The exact passage count and presentation remain a separate final-breach design
decision. The important promise is that no ordinary site completion is erased,
the player understands why the desktop is different, and the breach checkpoints
safely. Finn can resume after fatigue, a browser interruption, or an intentional
exit without replaying completed capstone work.

On success, Finn verifies and executes the revocation through the recovery tool
Amy prepared. The desktop returns to its stable state, the ten sites remain
secured, and the eleventh live file is preserved as proof of the attempted
breach. Chinmay receives:

`OUTSIDE ADMINISTRATOR ACCESS: REVOKED`

Finn receives **Smarter Than the Developer** because he learned the system well
enough to see what Chinmay's rushed code missed.

## Character progression

### Finn

Finn's competence is expressed through consequences, not dialogue that tells
him he is smart. He begins by following a repair tool, then notices inconsistent
behavior, preserves evidence, recognizes repeated patterns, and finally
verifies and executes the correct access decision.

### Amy

Amy's role grows from tool-builder to investigative partner:

- early: explains the safe desktop and recovery tools;
- middle: notices patterns and asks Finn to preserve evidence;
- late: interprets the assembled route and prepares containment;
- finale: prepares and safeguards the revocation tool while Finn verifies the
  route and runs it.

She never supplies a comprehension answer or repairs a site for Finn.

### Chinmay

Chinmay moves through four readable modes:

1. helpful CEO claiming the incident is under control;
2. confident explainer whose claims do not match the logs;
3. frustrated administrator reframing Finn's progress as a problem;
4. open antagonist attempting to preserve his access.

His dialogue should change at count-based global beats while each site retains
its own custom jokes and interruptions.

### Techno

Techno has a small campaign story told entirely through behavior. Early, she
chases her ball around the clean desktop. In the middle, she repeatedly places
it beside evidence that shares a connector or timestamp. Late, she guards the
Case File. During the breach, the ball becomes a reassuring stable object in an
unstable-looking desktop, but it never becomes a required puzzle item.

## Reward and adaptation language

The wrapper should not say that high scores let Finn skip work or that weaker
scores create extra work. Progress copy remains deliberately vague:

- `That repair held.`
- `More of the page came back than expected.`
- `Clean pass. The site gave up a bigger section.`
- `Signal stable. Keep the window open.`
- `Evidence saved.`

Site-specific game rules may turn an accepted reading outcome into a larger
visual jump. The hub records the resulting site state, not the hidden formula.
Difficulty selection, content variety, speech scoring, and campaign length must
remain separate decisions.

## Replay behavior

After the canonical campaign, secured sites can be replayed as recovered
archives without reopening Chinmay's outside access. Replay should prefer unseen
passage IDs and may use alternate local sabotage episodes, but it does not undo
the story ending or pretend the site was never secured.

The Case File can expose optional “alternate incident” stamps so repeat play
feels like discovering another weird version of the same archetype rather than
grinding the identical repair.

## Ownership boundaries

| Concern | Owner |
| --- | --- |
| Passage text, questions, provenance, rights, difficulty, speech aliases | Content Platform |
| Microphone, local transcript, alignment, pace, accuracy, live highlighting | Reading Engine |
| Theme-neutral accepted outcome, outcome-to-progress mapping, campaign state machine, case-selection policy, and count-based progression triggers | Game Rules |
| Passage history, campaign snapshot, evidence IDs, story flags, and last open window | Non-audio session state through stable layer interfaces |
| Site identities and local story configuration; Recovery desktop, Case File art, Amy, Chinmay, Techno, dialogue, and breach presentation | Internet Recovery 98 wrapper |

The campaign may persist passage IDs, site state, evidence IDs, story flags, and
the last open window. It must not persist raw voice recordings or transcripts.

## Design checks before implementation

- Does the hub feel like a funny place to return to rather than a chapter list?
- Can Finn understand Incoming, Recovering, and Secured without a tutorial wall?
- Does he notice a repaired site and new evidence before Amy comments on it?
- Does the third-file pattern feel suspicious without spoiling Chinmay's role?
- Can any remaining site carry late-game context without rewriting its local arc?
- Does `10 OF 10` feel like a genuine accomplishment before the breach begins?
- Is the final desktop corruption surprising precisely because the desktop was
  reliable for the preceding campaign?
- Does Amy enable the victory while Finn remains the person who earned it?
- Can every character layer be hidden without affecting reading, scoring, or
  comprehension?
