# Reading-source research for the nine proposed sites

## Research conclusion

Use official standardized-test specifications as a curriculum guide, not as a
text source. College Board describes literature, history/social studies,
humanities, science, evidence, inference, structure, purpose, and cross-text
reasoning. Official SAT and ACT passages/questions should not be copied without
permission. Select our own lawful source texts and write original questions.

The first review-only application of this research is the
[`nine-site sample passage sampler`](../../content/proposed-sites/SAMPLE_PASSAGES.md).
It provides one adapted sample per proposed non-WikiWhy site so reading
identity, rights metadata, question design, sensitivity, and speech risk can be
reviewed before full decks are commissioned.

## Preferred source classes

1. **Verified U.S. public-domain literature.** Project Gutenberg is a discovery
   and transcription source, but passage records should identify the underlying
   work/edition and omit Gutenberg headers, footers, and trademark material.
2. **Library of Congress collections with item/collection rights statements.**
3. **U.S.-government employee-authored work** from NASA, NOAA, NPS, FTC, USGS,
   or other agencies, after checking bylines and third-party notices.
4. **Explicit open licenses**, such as CC BY journal material or CC0 Smithsonian
   records, with required attribution/change notices.
5. **Human-reviewed original writing** for contemporary topics where clean
   reusable sources are unavailable.

Do not assume an entire website is public domain. Government sites can contain
contractor, partner, quoted, or third-party content. Smithsonian is not blanket
public domain; use only records marked CC0. Modern lyrics, performances, reviews,
and creator transcripts are usually poor reuse candidates.

## Twain finding

Project Gutenberg identifies both *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer* and
*Adventures of Huckleberry Finn* as public domain in the United States.

**Tom Sawyer:** candidate for MyCorner after excerpt-level review. The narrative
reflection around the whitewashing episode is interesting, but surrounding
dialogue may contain dialect, archaic contractions, stereotypes, or words that
speech recognition handles poorly.

**Huckleberry Finn:** do not use as a default first-deck source. The novel
intentionally uses multiple dialect systems and contains racial slurs and
historical material requiring careful context. Only a human-selected,
standard-spelling, sensitivity-reviewed excerpt that passes a real microphone
test should advance. *Life on the Mississippi* is the stronger first Twain
candidate for MapGuess.

## Candidate works and collections

### Argument and civic writing

- John Stuart Mill, *On Liberty*
- Mary Wollstonecraft, *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*
- Thoreau, *Civil Disobedience*
- Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk*
- paired Federalist and Anti-Federalist selections
- Douglass speeches and newspapers

### Voice, memoir, and identity

- Douglass, *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass*
- Harriet Jacobs, *Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl*
- Booker T. Washington, *Up from Slavery*
- Jane Addams, *Twenty Years at Hull-House*
- Zitkála-Šá, *American Indian Stories*
- Austen, Brontë, Shelley, Wilde, Chopin, Fitzgerald, and Jack London selections

### Story, drama, and narration

- *The Time Machine*
- selected Sherlock Holmes stories
- *Frankenstein*
- public-domain drama and oratory
- original documentary/video essays

### Science, geography, and consumer knowledge

- NASA science explainers and archival dispatches
- NOAA ocean, weather, climate, hydrography, and sound material
- NPS geology, ecology, history, and sound-science material
- FTC consumer, privacy, reviews, phishing, and dark-pattern guidance
- USGS cartographic material
- CC BY research/plain-language pairs

### Historical media

- Chronicling America newspapers, manually proofed against scans
- Frederick Douglass Newspapers
- Library of Congress letters, life histories, maps, and selected digitized books
- public-domain advertisements and catalogs

## Passage acceptance record

Every passage should eventually store:

- site, deck, and stable passage ID;
- author, title, publication year, edition, chapter/section, source URL, and date;
- rights basis and separate evidence URL;
- required attribution and whether text is verbatim or adapted;
- jurisdiction limits;
- original comprehension question and answer rationale;
- content/sensitivity review;
- speech-risk flags for names, dialect, numerals, symbols, foreign phrases,
  archaic spelling, OCR errors, and unusual punctuation;
- frozen source revision or hash;
- results of at least one real-microphone transcription test.
